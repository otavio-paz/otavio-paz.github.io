# Can machines think?

In this constantly changing post, I’ll do my best to define terms, acknowledge assumptions, discuss arguments, and give my opinions on the initial question and its implications. Most of them come from discussions I had in my CSCI 375 Ethics and Epistemology of AI with Prof. McCarrin in Oberlin College.

One thing that I found interesting about the many arguments discussed on this topic: it will eventually tell more about us, than just explaining the machines themselves: whether our mind is fully material or not, whether we have free will or not, what’s “human understanding”, and many more. Thus, and I think many fail on this understanding, the question posed no longer can be answered isolated from the realm of Computer Science.

Perhaps in the future I'll be adding a list of content with the different arguments and definitions.

- Stochastic Parrots

Right now, I want to focus on the idea of stochastic parrots discussed by Bender et al. (2021). I recommend reading the article because I will not be just summarizing its claims.

What makes the "stochastic parrot" critique so interesting is that many of the rebuttals to it seem to answer a weaker claim than the one Bender et al. were actually focusing. The easy rebutall is: but these system are not merely copying and pasting their training data with some probabilistic magic. Fair enough. With fine-tuning, post-training methods, LLM in fact can become more coherence, produce correct outputs and have some level of ground-truth checking given a certain context. That much is true to the extent I undertand, but I do not think this really settles the depper issue.

The stronger version of the parrots argument is not really about this probabilistic bird, I believe it's the way we see them. We have the tendency to enocunter fluent language and immediately infer mind, intention, understading, even personhood--for now I lack good sources to corrobate this claim. Oddly enough, one analogy that comes to my mind is from Frieren: Beyond Journey's End. The comparison may sound fastasticall (or foolish) but I think it gets at something real. In Frieren, demons, a race of human-eating monsters, can reproduce human speech with terrifying effectivness, not because they share human feeling, but because they learn what words to do humans. A demon might learn to say "mom" when is attacked, not out of love or empathy, but because it has learned that the word makes humans not killing it. In an analogous way, LLM can produce language associated with wisdom, honestly, or even care without possessng any of those qualities.
